Gopher Tortoise

(Gopherus polyphemus)

1. Biological Overview

The Gopher Tortoise is Alabama's only native tortoise, meaning it is a land-dwelling turtle. It is a keystone species because its deep burrows provide shelter for over $350$ other animal species. They have stumpy, elephant-like hind legs and powerful, shovel-shaped front legs for digging. Their status is federally Threatened west of the Mobile and Tombigbee Rivers.

2. Habitat & Geographical Location

Gopher Tortoises require dry, well-drained, sandy soils for digging their extensive burrows. In Alabama, populations are primarily concentrated in the coastal plain and longleaf pine ecosystems of the southern and southeastern counties, where the appropriate soil and sunny conditions exist. A typical burrow can be $15$ feet long and $6.5$ feet deep.

3. Track Identification (Land Print)

Key Identification Features:

  • Front Tracks: Large, distinctive, and splayed. They are wide and flat, often showing clear marks from the strong claws on the front feet, designed like shovels for earth-moving. The tracks are often toe-in.
  • Rear Tracks: Smaller, more compact, and rounded (resembling a miniature elephant's foot). The five short toes are less splayed than the front feet.
  • Gait: Alternating left/right footprints. The tracks are often paired with a clear, shallow drag mark from the plastron, but no deep body trough like a sea turtle.
