Flattened Musk Turtle

(Sternotherus depressus)

1. Biological Overview

This is a small, aquatic turtle endemic to Alabama (found nowhere else). Its namesake feature is its distinctly **flattened carapace** (less than $5$ inches long), an adaptation that allows it to slip into narrow rock crevices in streambeds for defense and hunting. They are shy, secretive bottom-dwellers that primarily feed on aquatic invertebrates like snails and mussels. They are listed as **Threatened**.

2. Habitat & Geographical Location

The Flattened Musk Turtle is restricted to the **upper Black Warrior River system** in west-central Alabama, specifically above the Bankhead Dam. They thrive in optimal habitats consisting of **free-flowing large creeks or small rivers** with alternating pools and riffles, an abundance of submerged boulders, and most importantly, **low silt loads** and excellent water quality.

3. Track Identification (Stream Bottom Crawl)

Key Identification Features:

  • **Print Size:** Extremely small, typically less than $5$ centimeters wide. Due to their small size and bottom-dwelling nature, clear prints are rare and are often found only in soft sand or fine silt.
  • **Foot Shape:** Shows impressions of **small, webbed feet** and slender claws. The turtle uses a "scurrying" movement along the bottom rather than a strong walking gait.
  • **Distinguishing Feature:** Prints are usually shallow and irregular. The best signs are often **slight trails in the fine stream-bottom sediment** in areas known to have low siltation and high rock/boulder cover.
