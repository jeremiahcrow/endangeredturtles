Loggerhead Sea Turtle

(Caretta caretta)

1. Biological Overview

The Loggerhead is named for its large head and powerful jaw muscles, which it uses to crush the shells of hard-shelled prey like conchs and crabs. They are the most frequently encountered nesting sea turtle in Alabama. Adults can reach up to $3.5$ feet in length and weigh $200$ to $350$ pounds. Their carapace is reddish-brown. They are listed as Threatened.

2. Habitat & Geographical Location

Loggerheads are found in temperate and tropical regions worldwide. In Alabama, their critical nesting habitat extends along the Gulf Coast beaches, particularly between Fort Morgan and Gulf Shores (Baldwin County). They prefer coastal waters of the Continental Shelf and estuarine areas for foraging, migrating thousands of miles between foraging grounds and nesting beaches.

3. Track Identification (Beach Crawl)

Key Identification Features:

  • Body Drag: The central trough left by the shell is wide and deep (up to $3$ feet across), giving the overall impression of a trench. This is much wider than the Kemp's Ridley track.
  • Flipper Marks (Gait): Tracks are asymmetrical or alternating, meaning the left front flipper moves forward, then the right front flipper. This creates distinct, comma-shaped flipper impressions that alternate on either side of the central body drag.
  • Distinguishing Feature: The alternating gait is the primary difference used to distinguish the Loggerhead track from the symmetrical track of the Kemp's Ridley.
