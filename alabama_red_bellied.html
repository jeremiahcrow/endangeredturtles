Alabama Red-bellied Turtle

(Pseudemys rubriventris / P. alabamensis)

1. Biological Overview

This turtle holds the distinction of being Alabama's official state reptile. It is a large freshwater turtle, growing up to 15 inches. Its name comes from the characteristic orange or reddish bottom shell (plastron). A key distinguishing feature is a prominent notch at the tip of the upper jaw. It is strictly herbivorous, feeding on aquatic vegetation. It is listed as Endangered.

2. Habitat & Geographical Location

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle is entirely restricted to the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta and associated major tributaries in Mobile and Baldwin Counties. It prefers shallow, vegetated backwaters of freshwater streams, rivers, bays, and bayous, especially those with extensive beds of submerged aquatic plants (its food source) and abundant basking sites (logs, banks).

3. Track Identification (Shoreline Print)

Key Identification Features:

  • Print Size/Shape: Relatively large for a freshwater turtle (up to 15 inches long). The prints clearly show the structure of their webbed feet and are often found in the soft mud or sand near the water's edge, particularly near basking logs.
  • Claw Marks (Males): Adult males have exceptionally long, sharp fore-claws used for courtship. These claws often leave distinct, thin, parallel lines or deep indentations in the substrate adjacent to the main footpad print.
  • Associated Sign: Often found adjacent to clear slide marks where the turtle has launched itself from a log or bank back into the water.
