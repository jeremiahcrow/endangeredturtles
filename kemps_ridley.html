Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

(Lepidochelys kempii)

1. Biological Overview

The Kemp's Ridley is the smallest of all sea turtle species, typically reaching about 2 feet in length and weighing up to 100 pounds. They have an oval, olive-gray carapace. Unique among sea turtles, they often exhibit "arribadas," or synchronized mass nesting events, though the primary nesting beaches are in Mexico and Texas. They are primarily carnivorous, feeding mainly on crabs and other crustaceans found on the ocean floor. Their status is Endangered.

2. Habitat & Geographical Location

This species inhabits the Gulf of Mexico and the coastal waters of the Atlantic Ocean. In Alabama, juveniles and non-nesting adults are often found foraging in coastal and bay waters of Mobile and Baldwin Counties. While nesting is rare in Alabama (unlike the Loggerhead), occasional nests have been documented, highlighting the importance of protecting the state's Gulf Coast beaches. They prefer shallow, coastal habitats.

3. Track Identification (Beach Crawl)

Key Identification Features:

  • Body Drag: The central trough left by the shell is relatively narrow (about 27 inches wide) compared to larger species, and often appears smooth or less defined.
  • Flipper Marks (Gait): The tracks are generally symmetrical or paired, meaning the left and right flippers touch the ground nearly simultaneously, creating clear, deep impressions on both sides of the central drag mark at the same point.
  • Claw Marks: Not typically distinct in the nesting crawl, as the fore-flippers are used like wings for propulsion.
